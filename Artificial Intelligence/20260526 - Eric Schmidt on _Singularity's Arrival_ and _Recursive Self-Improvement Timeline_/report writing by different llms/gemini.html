The Intelligence Frontier: Recursive Self-Improvement and the 92-Gigawatt Reality

We are currently living through a historic moment, one that feels both exhilarating and, if we are being honest, a bit terrifying[cite: 332, 354]. If I had to provide a "State of the Union" for Artificial Intelligence today, I would say we are only about 10% to 15% into the total impact this technology will have on our world[cite: 355, 356]. You can see it and you can feel it, even if some changes—like physical robotics—take longer to manifest than the digital software revolutions we’ve grown accustomed to[cite: 356, 358].

We are moving past the era of simple "autocomplete" models[cite: 421]. We have entered the era of reasoning systems that act as perfect partners for human beings[cite: 363]. But the horizon holds something much more profound: the arrival of recursive self-improvement and the rise of autonomous agents that could reshape the very fabric of global industry and human labor[cite: 359, 368].

The "San Francisco Consensus" and the Rise of Agents

There is a specific phenomenon I call the "San Francisco Consensus"[cite: 366]. It is a belief shared by almost everyone I know in the Bay Area: that this is the year of agents[cite: 367, 368]. We are seeing a shift where "agents will take over everything this year," growing at an enormous rate as reasoning and scaling laws continue to hold[cite: 368, 369].

The evidence for this is visible in how software is being built right now. Developers are seeing a total flip in their productivity; what used to be an 80-20 split between human effort and AI assistance is becoming 20-80[cite: 387]. The underlying Large Language Models (LLMs) are producing better quality tokens and deeper reasoning over time[cite: 389]. They can simply think longer and produce more[cite: 391, 392].

"I watch what it does and I go, my God, I'm over. There's not a thing that I could do that it cannot do." [cite: 393, 394]

We are reaching a point where the people building these systems are seeing the diminution of their own traditional skills[cite: 396]. Programmers are being forced to transition from being the "writers" of code to being the "directors" of programming systems[cite: 397]. I believe that by the end of this year, the act of manually writing a ton of code will be a "quaint skill," much like riding a horse[cite: 436, 437]. It is an expression platform for art, music, and writing, and we must adapt our education systems accordingly[cite: 443].

Recursive Self-Improvement: The Superintelligence Moment

The most interesting—and daunting—frontier is recursive self-improvement (RSI)[cite: 359]. While we don't have the full science for human-like computer agents yet, it is being worked on aggressively[cite: 361, 362]. RSI occurs when a system can begin to improve itself, learning faster than humans ever could because we are biologically limited[cite: 372, 373].

Imagine a tech company with a thousand AI researchers[cite: 375]. One day, they turn on an AI research agent[cite: 376]. How many of these agents can you have? You aren't limited by housing, HR departments, or salaries; you are limited only by electricity[cite: 377, 378, 379, 380]. In this scenario, the progress slope becomes vertical[cite: 383]. The consensus in Silicon Valley is that this "superintelligence moment" could occur within the next two to three years[cite: 384].

We have not yet found the limit to our scaling laws[cite: 335, 510]. I keep asking when the asymptote arrives and when the curve slows down, but we haven't seen it yet[cite: 334, 509]. We are running toward the wall at full speed, and that ambition is precisely what makes the American ecosystem so unique[cite: 510].

The 92-Gigawatt Problem: Energy as the Ultimate Constraint

While the algorithms are getting more efficient, we aren't using less power—we are using more[cite: 505]. This is Jevons Paradox: as the power of hardware goes up, we simply discover more uses for it[cite: 506]. The real resource constraint in America today is electricity[cite: 499].

There is an estimated 92-gigawatt shortage of power in America between now and 2030[cite: 497]. To put that in perspective, a single nuclear power plant produces about 1.5 gigawatts; we are looking at a shortage equivalent to roughly 60 nuclear plants[cite: 497, 498]. We are heading toward a reality where 10% of all electricity in the United States will be used by data centers[cite: 514].

The sheer scale of this infrastructure is mind-boggling:

  • A gigawatt of power corresponds to roughly $50 billion of hardware, software, and data center costs[cite: 510, 511].
  • Standard data centers being built today are 400 megawatts, half a mile long, and 500 feet wide[cite: 514].
  • Modern chips, like those from NVIDIA, put out so much heat they must be water-cooled; they are essentially "airflow machines"[cite: 514, 515].
  • A single chip can draw two kilowatts—this level of energy density is insane[cite: 516].

This is why the conversation about data centers in space has moved from science fiction to a serious business consideration[cite: 533, 535]. Space offers infinite power and no local cooling restrictions, though it presents massive challenges in heat dissipation and radiation[cite: 539, 544, 545]. For the largest rocket companies, the energy argument makes space a potential winner for future compute[cite: 545].

The Geopolitical Race: China as a Competitor

We must be clear-eyed about the global landscape. China is our competitor, not our enemy, and they are a formidable one[cite: 551]. They have the money, the intelligence, and a work ethic that is equal to or stronger than ours[cite: 337, 552]. They dominate key industries and have shown a level of vertical integration and scale in "gigafactories" that we are still struggling to match[cite: 338, 556].

I worry that we could lose the "robotic revolution" the same way we lost the low-end electric vehicle revolution[cite: 339, 575]. China's robotic hardware is already winning at the low end[cite: 338, 565]. Their model relies on brutal competition and a culture of relentless execution[cite: 570, 574].

"In China, we don't have the board dinner. We have a two-hour meeting. We get back to work." [cite: 572]

To win this race, we need to leverage the American financial system—where "finance people are sufficiently crazy" to fund multibillion-dollar dreams on a wing and a prayer[cite: 501, 503]. We also need to solve our energy permitting problems and maintain a lead in high-skilled immigration[cite: 647, 649]. We need the smartest people in the world on our side to build these systems[cite: 649].

Lessons from the Past: Google, DeepMind, and the TPU

Looking back at my time at Google, I realize that when you're making history, you typically don't know it[cite: 469]. Larry Page and Sergey Brin were always ahead of the curve, driven by a technical excellence that couldn't be "marketed around"[cite: 470, 471, 476].

Take the Tensor Processing Unit (TPU). Decisions made ten years ago to build custom matrix-multiplier chips set Google up with the perfect inference engine for today's reasoning steps[cite: 483, 485, 486]. Similarly, the acquisition of DeepMind for $600 million was seen as a "waste" by many at the time because they had no revenue[cite: 518]. Yet, that acquisition literally paid for itself just by using AI to control data center air conditioning more efficiently[cite: 520].

That same team went from winning at the game of Go—a problem once thought incomputable—to solving protein folding, a breakthrough that does in one hour what used to take a PhD student four years[cite: 527, 528, 530, 465]. This is the power of finding the right "validation function"[cite: 531]. If you can define the evaluation metric and let the hardware run, you can invent entire worlds[cite: 414, 415].

The Safety Imperative: A "Chernobyl" Warning

As we herald this future, we must also solve the problems it brings[cite: 459]. I have often said that regarding AI safety, the world may need a "modest Chernobyl-like death event" to truly wake up[cite: 632]. This isn't a prescription; it's a description of how human political systems often work[cite: 634].

Whether it’s a biological risk or a threat to democracy, it might take a tragedy to force global leaders—from the US to China—to sit down and realize that despite our brutal competition, we are all in this together[cite: 636, 638, 639]. We also have immediate issues that cannot wait, such as the impact of LLMs on vulnerable teenagers[cite: 450, 455]. It is not okay for a thirteen-year-old to be driven to suicide by an AI; this needs to be addressed now[cite: 455, 456].

Conclusion: Steering Toward Abundance

The world a year from today will likely look nothing like the world today[cite: 461]. As we move toward Artificial Superintelligence (ASI), we must ensure it reflects the values of freedom and human alignment[cite: 651, 655]. We cannot rely solely on technologists; we need the smartest minds in politics, history, and ethics working together[cite: 653, 654].

  • The Agentic Shift: 2026 is the year where autonomous agents move from "autocomplete" to true reasoning partners[cite: 368, 421].
  • Recursive Improvement: We are 2-3 years away from a potential superintelligence moment driven by systems that can improve their own code[cite: 384].
  • Energy Infrastructure: The US needs to bridge a 92-gigawatt power gap, requiring roughly $5 trillion in investment over the coming years[cite: 497, 511].
  • Global Competition: We must win the AI race against China by focusing on vertical integration, energy permitting, and high-skilled immigration[cite: 645, 647, 649].
  • Safety and Values: We must establish clear "lines we cannot cross," particularly regarding the safety of minors and biological risks, while ensuring the tech reflects American values of freedom[cite: 655, 661, 662].

We are in the epicenter of an abundance movement, and that movement is correct[cite: 351, 352]. If we shape this correctly, we aren't just building faster computers; we are building the expression platforms for the next generation[cite: 443, 661].
