We Are 10–15% Into the AI Revolution: Recursive Self-Improvement, the 92‑Gigawatt Problem, and the Race for Abundance

We’re living through a historic moment. I’ve been in technology for decades—through the rise of Unix, the early internet, the founding of Google—and what we’re witnessing today is unlike anything I’ve seen before. The next thing that is both fascinating and terrifying is recursive self-improvement. It’s not here yet, but the trajectory is clear. The real question everyone in the labs keeps asking: “When does the asymptote arrive and when does the curve slow down?” The honest answer? We haven’t found the limit yet.

The Coming of Recursive Self-Improvement

In San Francisco, there’s a consensus—I call it the “San Francisco consensus”—that we’re on the cusp of something unprecedented. The idea is simple: once you have systems capable of recursive self-improvement, intelligence begins to learn on its own, faster than any biological mind. Why? Because we’re limited by biology; silicon is not. As I’ve put it, “In this thinking, once you have recursive self-improvement, where the system can begin to improve itself, you have intelligence learning on its own. And in this argument, it will learn faster than we can because we're biologically limited.”

Imagine a tech company with a thousand brilliant AI researchers. Then imagine they turn on AI research agents. Suddenly you’re not limited by housing, HR, or salaries—only by electricity. “How many AI research agents do you have? Well, as many as you're limited by electricity. Right? You don't have to feed them. They don't need housing. There's no more housing in San Francisco. All that kind of stuff.” You could have a million agents, all iterating on reasoning metrics, all pushing the frontier while you sleep. The belief here is that this superintelligence moment arrives within two to three years.

“The belief in San Francisco is this occurs within two to three years.”

From Programmer to Director of Intelligent Systems

I was a programmer in high school—back when you had to build everything from scratch. Now I watch what today’s models can do and I think: “My God, I'm over. There's not a thing that I could do that it cannot do.” When they wrote a C compiler in Rust, I thought, it’s over. Declare success. That shift is forcing the best programmers to evolve: from writing every line of code to being directors of programming systems.

Here’s a story that captures the moment. I was talking to a young developer working on UIs. He told me: “I write the spec of what I want, and then I write a test function, an evaluation function, and then I turn it on.” I asked what time. Seven o’clock in the evening. Then he has dinner with his wife, goes to sleep, wakes up at four in the morning, and sees what’s been invented. “This stuff would have taken me six months and ten programmers at Google to do the same thing. This poor guy is sleeping.” That’s the power we now have. If you can define an evaluation function and you have enough hardware, you’re inventing worlds overnight.

This also means that the very top programmers—those with deep mathematical reasoning skills—become even more valuable. They become the architects who orchestrate armies of agents. But for the rest, the nature of work is shifting permanently. It’s not that coding disappears; it becomes a high-level act of composition.

The Great Constraint: 92 Gigawatts and the Electricity Wall

When I testified before Congress, I pointed to a number that many found staggering: “an estimated 92 gigawatts shortage of power in America between now and 2030.” To put that in perspective, a nuclear power plant is about 1.5 gigawatts. That’s the equivalent of 60 nuclear plants—and we’re building essentially zero or one. The real resource constraint for AI is not chips, not talent—it’s electricity.

Every major data center being built today is a half-mile long, 500 feet wide, consuming upwards of 400 megawatts. They are essentially airflow machines, now with water-cooled chips because the heat is lethal. The standard data center I used to build at Google seems tiny by comparison. And yet, because of Jevons paradox—greater efficiency leads to more demand—we’ll keep needing more. “As the power of the hardware goes up, as the algorithms become more efficient, you don't need less power. You need even more power and even more computers because we discover new uses.” The current projection: 10% of all electricity in the United States will go to data centers.

The good news? American capitalism is uniquely positioned to meet this. “We have the universities, we have the smart people, we have the economics and we have these amazing finance people who will give all of us billions and billions of dollars on a wing and a prayer. I mean, there's no country where the finance people are sufficiently crazy to do that.” We can raise the capital—trillions over five years—to solve the power problem. But it requires political will and a willingness to build at a scale we’ve never seen.

China, Robotics, and the Battle for Physical AI

We need to talk about competition. Not an enemy—a competitor. China has lots of money, brilliant people, and a work ethic that is equal or stronger than ours. They dominate key industries, and the one that keeps me up at night is robotics. I wrote recently that we’re at risk of losing the robotic revolution the same way we lost the low‑end electric vehicle revolution. That was an error. “We somehow decided it was okay for them to dominate the electric vehicle industry. This was an error. To be very clear, it's an error.” Spend time outside the U.S. in Chinese cars—trust me, they are real competitors.

Now look at robotics. Actuators, motors, the brain—the entire stack. The electric vehicle industry produces the same kinds of motors and systems. China has built expertise at scale that we don’t. At the moment, “it sure looks to me like the robotic hardware of China is the winner at the low end.” Watch Unitree’s robots dancing with humans. They’re one of many, and the competition there is brutal—unlike the board‑dinner culture we’re used to. The Chinese approach is: two‑hour meeting, back to work. No preamble. That intensity, combined with their supply chain and vertical integration, makes them a formidable force.

Elon Musk pioneered vertical integration in our country—building everything from the ground up to drive costs down. To achieve abundance, to bring prices down, that’s exactly what’s required. The question is: can we match the speed and scale of China’s robotics push before the next industrial revolution slips through our fingers?

The DeepMind Story: Patience, Go, and Protein Folding

Everyone asks how we ended up here. Let me tell you a story. When we acquired DeepMind for roughly $600 million, people thought we were crazy. “Why would you waste $600 million on a zero‑revenue AI that only plays Go?” Years later, it turned out that the acquisition paid for itself just by optimizing data‑center air conditioning more efficiently. But the real payoff was deeper. In 2016, when DeepMind’s team went to Korea to beat the world champion at Go, I watched from the sidelines. The Koreans were confident they’d crush the computer. In the Google room, it was quiet—just a monitor showing a probability. It started at 50‑50, then 51%, then 52%. Finally it climbed to “infinity” in the algorithm’s mind. The humans were crushed. I realized then the genius of Demis Hassabis and his team.

They didn’t stop at Go. They took the same team and applied it to protein folding—a problem that had consumed PhD students for years. “It does the work in an hour that used to take a PhD student four years. It's like 300 million times more efficient.” That’s the power of taking a general‑purpose learning system and pointing it at a well‑defined validation function. It’s also a perfect example of the patience of capital: we let them grow, we didn’t force immediate revenue, and they changed biology forever.

Shaping the Future: Values, Safety, and Abundance

I’m often asked about safety. I’ve said before that the world may need a modest “Chernobyl‑like” event to truly wake up to the dangers. I say that as a description of human nature, not a prescription. We have real dangers: biological threats, the impact on teenagers, the destabilization of democracies. “It is not OK for 13-year-olds to be committing suicide because of an LLM. It's just not OK. It needs to be addressed right now.” Those are lines we cannot cross.

At the same time, I don’t want to slow down American innovation. We need to win the AI race—but win it with our values intact. “I want the system that we build in America to reflect American values, the values of freedom and freedom of speech, and freedom of association, all those things you learned in college and elementary school.” We can shape this without breaking the genius of our system. It requires political will, cross‑industry collaboration, and an understanding that the fastest learner wins—not just technically, but ethically.

My advice? Right now, universities should be teaching every freshman how to use these tools. Prompt engineering, evaluation functions, agent orchestration—that should be the foundation of education. And as a country, we need to open our doors to the world’s brightest minds. High‑skill immigration is our superpower. The people who will build the next generation of AI should be here, on our side, working toward abundance.

Conclusion: The Road to Superintelligence and Abundance

  • Recursive self-improvement is coming. The science isn’t fully proven yet, but the frontier labs are leapfrogging each other weekly. Expect the first real “self‑improving” systems within 2–3 years—and they will accelerate faster than humans can follow.
  • Electricity is the bottleneck of the decade. We need 92 gigawatts of new power by 2030—equivalent to 60 nuclear plants. Solving this will require massive investment, regulatory reform, and a willingness to build at unprecedented scale.
  • China is our fiercest competitor in physical AI. They’ve already won the low‑end EV race; robotics is next unless we match their vertical integration, work ethic, and supply‑chain mastery.
  • Human roles are transforming, not disappearing. Top programmers become directors of AI agents. The ability to define evaluation functions and orchestrate complex systems will be the most valuable skill of the coming era.
  • Safety requires proactive shaping, not slowing down. We must solve the underage safety crisis, prevent biological misuse, and embed American values of freedom and association into the architecture of our AI systems.
  • The abundance movement is correct. If we navigate this transition wisely—with massive capital, open immigration, and a relentless focus on making technology affordable—AI can deliver an era of abundance beyond anything we’ve imagined.

We are 10–15% into the impacts of this revolution. The next few years will be unlike anything we’ve experienced. Let’s make sure we build the future we actually want: one defined by human flourishing, not by our fears. The tools are here. The question is whether we have the wisdom to use them well.

— Eric Schmidt, reflections on the state of AI, energy, and the race toward recursive self‑improvement.
